# The Rings of Power — Episode Companion

An interactive, spoiler-aware companion to *The Lord of the Rings: The Rings of Power*.

This project began as a storyline tracker, but the design evolved after working through the series in detail. The show does not divide cleanly into stable storylines: plots merge and split, relationships cross regions, and many important scenes matter because of character, theme, history, or setup rather than because they advance one obvious plot thread.

The organizing principle is therefore:

> **Season → Episode → Expandable sections → Story detail and contextual lore**

This document records the editorial decisions behind that structure so future seasons can be added consistently.

---

## 1. The episode is the primary unit

Every piece of story coverage belongs first to the episode in which it occurs.

Do **not** force scenes into permanent cross-season or cross-episode storyline categories. A storyline that looks coherent in one episode may merge with another, split apart, change characters, or stop being the most useful way to understand what is happening.

The episode page should instead contain several expandable sections describing the distinct things that matter in that episode.

Examples:

- Galadriel refuses to let the hunt end
- Elrond stands between friendship and politics
- Nori cannot stop looking beyond the familiar
- Arondir and Bronwyn cross a boundary their communities do not trust
- Something is wrong beneath the apparent peace

Sections should begin **collapsed** so the reader can scan the shape of the episode before choosing what to open.

Episode navigation should remain compact (`S1E1`, `S1E2`, etc.) rather than using full episode titles in the selector.

---

## 2. Split sections by meaning, not by location or cast

A section should represent one coherent dramatic, emotional, or thematic development.

Two events in the same location do not automatically belong together. Likewise, one character may appropriately appear in several sections during the same episode.

Create separate sections when combining material would cause one important idea to bury another.

For example, in S1E1 the Southlands material is better divided into:

1. **Arondir and Bronwyn's relationship and the inherited hostility between Elves and Southlanders**
2. **The first signs that something dangerous is spreading: the black milk, Hordern, and Theo's sword**

The sword is important to the larger plot, but presenting it as the main subject of the Southlands introduction would understate the relationship and social conflict that dominate those scenes.

Do not create artificial sections merely to achieve symmetry. Some episodes will naturally need more sections than others.

---

## 3. Cover more than plot mechanics

The companion should answer not only **what happened**, but also **what the episode is establishing**.

Important material includes:

- character motivation
- friendships and romances
- family relationships
- political pressures
- cultural conflict
- changes in trust
- choices that reveal personality
- recurring fears or desires
- consequences that shape later behavior

For example:

- Elrond's early position between his friendship with Galadriel and his role within Gil-galad's political world matters because the tension between personal trust and public duty continues through his later relationships.
- Nori's curiosity, compassion, and difficulty accepting caution as an absolute rule are essential before she chooses to help the Stranger.
- Arondir and Bronwyn's romance is not background decoration. It affects Arondir's decision to remain in the Southlands, his attachment to its people, his developing relationship with Theo, and major choices made by both Arondir and Bronwyn.

A useful test is:

> **If this material were removed, would a viewer understand what happened but miss why a later choice feels emotionally important?**

If yes, it probably belongs in the companion.

---

## 4. Stay grounded in what the show has actually presented

The episode sections should describe what the viewer can reasonably understand **at that point in the series**.

Do not use later knowledge to turn early scenes into theories or winks at the audience.

For example, early material about the Stranger should discuss:

- what Nori sees
- what the Stranger does
- how Nori responds
- how their relationship develops

It should **not** repeatedly suggest a future identity before the show itself makes that connection sufficiently explicit.

Likewise, Nori's kindness may become interesting in retrospect, but an early episode entry should not tell the reader what that kindness might eventually explain. The companion should help readers notice what the show presents and allow them to form their own conclusions.

### Editorial rule

> **Describe evidence before interpretation, and never use interpretation to smuggle in a future reveal.**

---

## 5. Spoilers: protect dramatic revelations, not all cultural knowledge

The spoiler system is not based on the idea that every fact about Tolkien's world must remain hidden until the series states it.

There are three useful categories.

### A. Foundational or widely known lore

Broad knowledge familiar from *The Lord of the Rings* films or general popular culture can usually be available early when it adds useful context.

Examples:

- Sauron ultimately creates the One Ring.
- Mordor becomes Sauron's realm.
- Galadriel becomes the Lady of Lórien.
- Númenor eventually falls.

This is similar to watching a prequel while knowing Anakin Skywalker becomes Darth Vader: knowing the destination can create dread and dramatic irony. The suspense lies in **how the story gets there**.

### B. Contextual Tolkien lore

Background from Tolkien's wider writings may be introduced when it helps the viewer understand a character, people, place, object, or adaptation choice.

Examples:

- Galadriel's age, losses, pride, and long history
- the rarity of love between Elves and mortals
- Elrond's ancestry
- the history of Númenor
- the three ancient Hobbit-kindreds
- the unresolved traditions concerning the origin of Orcs

This material should deepen the story without implying that Tolkien explicitly wrote the show's exact version of events.

### C. Show-specific revelations

Protect these strictly until the relevant episode has been watched.

Examples include:

- secret identities
- unexpected deaths
- betrayals
- the hidden purpose of an object or location
- the connection between mysteries that the show intentionally keeps separate
- the exact mechanism behind a major event

The Broken Sword is a key example. Before S1E6, the viewer may know that it bears Sauron's mark, reacts to blood, and is important to Adar's forces. The companion must **not** reveal or strongly hint at its true function, because the moment when the sword, dam, tunnels, and eruption suddenly connect is one of the show's major reveals.

### Core spoiler principle

> **Known destination is often acceptable. Hidden route is not.**

---

## 6. Lore should enrich, not steer

Lore pop-ups are an optional deeper layer. They should let a curious reader descend into the history of Middle-earth without making the main episode text feel like a textbook.

Useful labels include:

- **Tolkien background** — established wider lore
- **Known history** — broad future history familiar to many viewers
- **Adaptation note** — a clear difference or compression introduced by the series
- **Adaptation lens** — a careful interpretation of how the series may be drawing on broader or conflicting Tolkien traditions

Be especially cautious with adaptation interpretation. It should explain a plausible literary connection without claiming certainty about the writers' intentions.

Prefer:

> Tolkien wrote multiple versions of Galadriel's early motivations, including traditions emphasizing pride, ambition, and a desire for a realm of her own. The series appears to dramatize some of that unresolved tradition through her restlessness.

Avoid:

> The writers made Galadriel do this because Tolkien said...

unless there is strong direct evidence for that claim.

---

## 7. Character relationships deserve first-class treatment

Relationships often explain more than plot summaries do.

Track how relationships change over time, including:

- affection
- distrust
- obligation
- rivalry
- grief
- mentorship
- political dependence
- family tension

Do not reduce a relationship to a label such as `friend`, `romance`, or `enemy`. The episode sections should show **what the relationship causes people to do**.

The Arondir–Bronwyn relationship is a model example. Its importance includes:

- the exceptional nature of an Elf–mortal romance
- the hostility between their communities
- Arondir returning to Bronwyn instead of simply leaving with his company
- Bronwyn's trust in Arondir
- Arondir's growing attachment to the Southlanders
- Theo's conflicted response to the Elf close to his mother
- the ways Arondir and Bronwyn repeatedly affect one another's decisions

When later production realities change a story, preserve the emotional importance of what came before rather than retroactively treating it as disposable.

---

## 8. Characters are people; identities are presentations

Use one canonical entity for a person even when that person uses different names, titles, disguises, or identities.

The interface may display different identities according to the viewer's spoiler position, but relationships and appearances should remain attached to the same underlying entity.

For example, Sauron's presentation can change over time without fragmenting him into unrelated database characters.

This allows:

- spoiler-aware names
- continuity of relationships
- unified search
- complete appearance history
- later identities without duplicate entities

The same pattern should be reused for future characters when appropriate.

---

## 9. Browse entries must also obey the spoiler philosophy

Spoiler protection applies to:

- episode sections
- character profiles
- places
- races and peoples
- objects
- lore pop-ups
- search results
- aliases and identity names

An object description must not reveal more than the episode page would.

Use staged summaries when knowledge changes over time. A later `revealAt` entry may add or replace information once the viewer reaches the relevant episode.

Search aliases must also be spoiler-aware where necessary. A hidden identity should not be discoverable merely because a user searched for its later name.

---

## 10. Automatic entity links must match whole terms

The app automatically links known characters, peoples, places, and objects inside prose.

Matches must respect word boundaries. Short names must never link when they merely appear inside another word—for example, `Men` must not become a link inside `fragment`.

When adding new entities or aliases, consider whether the term is so short or common that automatic linking could create false matches.

---

## 11. Harfoots and other peoples should receive real cultural context

Peoples should not exist only as labels in the Browse screen.

Where Tolkien provides relevant background, include it.

For the Harfoots, useful context includes:

- they are one of the three ancient Hobbit-kindreds, alongside Stoors and Fallohides
- Tolkien describes them as the most numerous of the three
- they were the first of those groups to migrate west into Eriador
- Tolkien describes them as browner of skin than the other Hobbit-kindreds
- later Hobbit populations emerged through migration and intermingling rather than descending from one perfectly isolated group
- the show's wandering Second Age Harfoots are not simply the already-settled Shire society seen thousands of years later

Present this as world-building, not as an argument about modern casting discourse.

---

## 12. Writing style

The companion should be:

- clear enough for someone who is having trouble following the show
- rich enough to reward someone who already understands the plot
- warm toward the material without becoming promotional
- willing to distinguish show canon, Tolkien lore, and interpretation
- specific about motivation without pretending to know unspoken thoughts with certainty

Prefer concrete language.

Instead of:

> This is important for later.

Explain what is already important now.

Instead of:

> This foreshadows a major revelation.

Describe the present mystery and protect the revelation.

Instead of overusing phrases such as `this sets up`, let the reader see the connection through carefully chosen facts.

---

## 13. Adding a new episode

For each newly released episode:

1. **Watch or verify the complete episode story.** Do not build only from a short synopsis.
2. **List the distinct dramatic developments.** Do not begin by assigning permanent storylines.
3. **Create expandable sections by meaning.** Split sections when different emotional or thematic material would otherwise compete.
4. **Include character and relationship development.** Ask what changed internally or relationally, not only what physically happened.
5. **Check every sentence against the spoiler limit.** Do not use later knowledge accidentally.
6. **Add lore only where it genuinely clarifies the moment.** Avoid constant pop-ups and theory-signaling.
7. **Update Browse entities with staged knowledge.** Characters, places, peoples, and objects may need new summaries at the new reveal point.
8. **Check identities and aliases.** Add new presentations to existing canonical entities when appropriate rather than creating duplicates.
9. **Test automatic links.** Watch especially for short names and common words.
10. **Read the episode page as a first-time viewer.** Ask whether it helps understanding while preserving the experience of discovery.

---

## 14. Adding a new season

A new season should extend the same episode-first model rather than inventing a new navigation structure.

Before release:

- add the season and episode navigation shell
- preserve previous spoiler limits
- keep S1E1 as the default spoiler limit for a genuinely new visitor unless the product design is intentionally changed

As episodes release:

- add episode sections incrementally
- expand existing character and world entries through staged reveal data
- preserve canonical character identities across seasons
- revisit older material only when correcting an error or adding non-spoilery context; do not rewrite earlier entries so they suddenly know the future

The companion should feel as though it is accompanying the viewer through the series in order, even after many seasons have been added.

---

## 15. Product behavior worth preserving

Current design decisions:

- Primary navigation: **Episodes | Browse**
- Episode selector uses compact labels such as `S1E1`
- Episode sections start collapsed
- First-time spoiler limit defaults to `S1E1`
- Refreshing the main app returns the selected episode to `S1E1` while preserving the user's spoiler setting
- Shared/deep links may intentionally select a specific episode or spoiler state
- Lore and entity details open as lightweight bottom-sheet pop-ups on mobile
- The project remains static and GitHub Pages friendly: plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript with no required build step

---

## Guiding principle

The companion exists to help someone understand and appreciate the story **without taking discovery away from them**.

It should preserve both kinds of pleasure that a prequel can offer:

1. the dread and meaning that come from knowing the broad history of Middle-earth, and
2. the surprise of discovering exactly how this particular story chooses to get there.

When those goals conflict, protect the experience of the episode first.
